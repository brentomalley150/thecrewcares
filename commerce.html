New feature · Proposal

Nova knows what the crew needs — and where to get it

After a doctor's visit or medication change, Nova suggests products that genuinely help — pill organizers, BP monitors, grab bars. One tap to Amazon with full disclosure. The commerce model that doesn't compromise the brand.

Context-aware suggestions Amazon affiliate Full transparency
The short version

Nova proactively suggests care products when context warrants it — a pill organizer after a dose change, a BP monitor after a cardiology visit. Suggestions include clear reasoning ("recommended after Dr. Martinez's note"), Amazon links with an openly disclosed affiliate relationship, and a crew shopping list so the family coordinates who's buying what. The commerce earns revenue without turning the app into an ad platform.

Why this works for CrewCares specifically

Amazon affiliate integration is common. Doing it in family caregiving without feeling gross is the actual product question. Three things make Nova's approach different:

1

Context-driven, not interest-driven

Suggestions come from what the crew is actually doing — a doctor's visit, a new prescription. Not "people who liked this also bought" Amazon-style recommendations.

2

The AI is already trusted

Users already delegate tasks and scheduling to Nova. Asking Nova "what should I get for this?" is a natural extension of that relationship, not a new ad surface.

3

Fully disclosed, every time

"We earn a small referral fee" shown on every suggestion. Competitors hide this. We make it part of the feature. Transparency becomes a moat.

How it works

Concrete walkthrough. It's November 8th. Dad had his cardiology follow-up this morning. Dr. Martinez adjusted his blood pressure medication.

1

Nova picks up on the context

The appointment was on the calendar. The medication change was discussed in crew chat. When Brent opens the app the next morning, Nova has already synthesized the situation.

Nova card: "After Dad's cardiology visit, these might help"
2

Each product has a reason

Not a generic product grid. Each item includes one sentence explaining why — and the reason ties to the specific care situation, not to the crew's purchase history.

EZY DOSE 7-Day Pill Organizer
"Recommended for the new dose schedule Dr. Martinez prescribed"

Omron Silver BP Monitor
"Approved by AHA; syncs readings so the crew can track trends"
3

Two-way choice on every item

Every product has two buttons: "Buy on Amazon" (deep-link with affiliate tag) and "+ List" (add to the crew shopping list so someone else can pick it up locally). The app is agnostic about where the purchase happens.

4

Disclosure is inline, not buried

Every Nova recommendation card has a visible footer: "We earn a small referral fee when you buy through Amazon — it helps keep CrewCares running." Not in Terms of Service. Right on the recommendation.

ⓘ We earn a small referral fee when you buy through Amazon — it helps keep CrewCares running. Prices and availability are set by Amazon.
5

The crew shopping list is the real coordination layer

Even when Nova suggests Amazon, the list shows who added the item, who's buying, and where. Sarah added "low-sodium broth" — she'll grab it at Whole Foods. Linda's picking up adult briefs at CVS. Nova's pill organizer suggestion is on Amazon. All coordinated in one place.

Honest note on the math

These numbers are deliberately conservative. Real affiliate revenue depends heavily on category mix (health products pay less than home goods), conversion rates (caregivers are price-sensitive and shop around), and return rates (Amazon returns reduce payout). The model is real, but it's not going to be the primary business. Treat it as a supplementary stream, not a pitch headline.

What could go right — and wrong

Every feature has tradeoffs. Being honest about both is how we don't get surprised in investor Q&A.

What could go right

If this works, it compounds

  • Feature moat deepens. Nova becomes more useful the more it knows — crew history, care context, product preferences. Hard for competitors to copy.
  • Meaningful secondary revenue. Even conservative math shows $4M+ possible at 500K crews. Diversifies beyond subscription.
  • It's ethically differentiated. Caring Village hasn't done this. Full disclosure + context-driven suggestions + non-exclusive buying = a position competitors can't easily match without looking dishonest by comparison.
  • Data flywheel. What crews actually buy informs what Nova recommends next time. Category by category, the recommendations get sharper.
What could go wrong

Real risks to the brand

  • Users could feel surveilled. "Nova knows I had a doctor's visit." The feature depends on reading calendar + chat. Privacy framing has to be tight.
  • Bad recommendations erode trust fast. One generic or inappropriate product suggestion ("buy a wheelchair") after a routine checkup and the whole AI feels creepy.
  • Investor perception. Affiliate revenue can read as "no real business model yet." Has to be framed as one of multiple revenue streams, not a primary thesis.
  • Amazon dependency. If Amazon changes the affiliate program (they periodically cut rates), the feature economics shift. Diversify vendors over time.

V1 scope vs. later

Clear boundaries. What ships first is narrow on purpose.

V1 ships with

Manual curation, narrow scope

  • Nova recommends products from a hand-curated list of ~50 caregiving items tied to specific care contexts (medications, mobility, monitoring)
  • Recommendations triggered by explicit events — "post-appointment," "new prescription," "mobility concern mentioned"
  • Amazon affiliate deep-links; inline disclosure on every card
  • Crew shopping list as a separate screen, accessible from home and menu
  • "+List" option on every recommendation so users can coordinate non-Amazon purchases
  • Dismiss button per recommendation; full feature on/off in settings
Later releases

AI-authored, multi-vendor

  • Nova generates product suggestions dynamically based on full care context
  • Multi-vendor affiliate (Walmart, Target, CVS, Chewy for pet meds)
  • Price-comparison across vendors; "local pickup" integration where available
  • "Subscribe & Save" setup for recurring items (adult briefs, supplements)
  • Category expansion: home modifications (grab bars, bathroom aids), durable medical equipment
  • Partnership model: pharmacy chains, home health suppliers for higher margin categories

Risks we're designing around

How we prevent the predictable failure modes.

The "my AI is upselling me" feeling

Mitigation: every suggestion explains why specifically. If Nova can't articulate a care reason for recommending the product, it shouldn't surface. Recommendation generation starts narrow (curated list) and expands only with evidence of accuracy.

Privacy of chat + calendar data

Mitigation: Nova's product recommendations can be toggled off entirely in settings (on by default but prominently toggleable). Nova only uses care-relevant signals — doctor appointments, medication changes, explicit care discussion. Not birthdays, not gift shopping, not personal conversation.

Wrong recommendation damages trust in Nova broadly

Mitigation: V1 curation is tight (~50 items). Every item on the list is vetted for quality, ratings > 4.2, and caregiver relevance. No long-tail products. Better to have fewer, well-matched suggestions than broad coverage with misses.

Amazon changes the program

Mitigation: architect the recommendation engine to be vendor-agnostic from day one. Amazon is V1 because it's the easiest integration with broadest catalog. V2 adds Walmart and pharmacy chains. If Amazon cuts rates, we shift traffic.

Investor perception of affiliate as "not a real business"

Mitigation: frame this as one of four revenue lines — subscription (primary), affiliate commerce (secondary), enterprise / employer benefits (future), partnerships (future). Show the math but don't lead with it.

The investor framing

How to talk about this in a pitch without it undermining the company story.

It reinforces the AI story

Nova is already our differentiator. Commerce is an expression of Nova becoming more useful — not a separate feature bolted on. Strengthens the thesis that AI is our wedge.

It's a secondary revenue stream

Subscription stays primary. Affiliate is additive. Investors like to see multi-line revenue models, especially for consumer apps where subscription alone is hard.

Transparency is the moat

"We earn a small referral fee" is a feature, not a disclosure. Competitors who hide their affiliate relationships look dishonest by comparison. We own the trustworthy position.
